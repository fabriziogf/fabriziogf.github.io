---
title: "Rockford 70.3 Race Report"
---

Rockford was always going to be a complicated race to read. Five weeks out from Ironman Lake Placid, with CTL at 140 and a training block that peaked two weeks ago, the goal was never to peak here. It was to execute cleanly at controlled effort and come out the other side with fitness intact.

That is mostly what happened. I had a good race — solid across all three disciplines, personal records on the bike and run, and a finish that felt like a credible effort rather than a blow-up. But I also felt flat for most of it, in the specific way that a body mid-Ironman build feels flat: legs that are fit but not fresh, a mental edge that takes more work to find than it should. That is a training state, not a fitness problem.

---

## Swim

The swim came in at 22:19 for 1,900m — a 1:10/100m average. The intensity factor of 1.25 reflects the hard effort relative to aerobic threshold, which is exactly what an open-water race start demands. You go out hard, find your feet, and settle.

There is not much more to say about the swim. It was clean, it was fast enough, and I came out of the water ready to bike.

---

## Bike

The bike is where the numbers tell the clearest story. 90.4 km in 2:15:08, averaging 40.1 km/h with 614m of elevation gain. Average power 257W, normalized power 261W, intensity factor 0.80.

An IF of 0.80 on a 70.3 bike is deliberate. The textbook guidance for a 70.3 bike split is IF 0.83–0.88 if you want to race the run. I was lower than that — intentionally. Racing through a training block five weeks before an Ironman, there was no reason to be a hero on the bike and wreck the run or, worse, dig a fatigue hole I would spend the next two weeks climbing out of. The priority was execution over performance.

Ten personal records on the bike segments despite the conservative pacing is a useful signal: the fitness built over the past four months is real, and it is translating even when I am not trying to chase numbers.

Average cadence of 87 rpm and heart rate sitting between 132–156 (avg 146) suggests the effort was sustainable. Temperature was 19.7°C — good racing conditions.

---

## Run

The run was 21.0 km in 1:28:50 — 4:14/km average pace, heart rate averaging 148 with a max of 160. Four personal records. RPE was 9 out of 10.

That RPE is the most honest data point from the day. A 9 effort at 4:14/km pace is a strong result in absolute terms. But in relative terms — with the fitness I am carrying into this race — a 9 to produce that pace tells me the legs were working harder than they should have been for that speed. That is the flatness manifesting. Fresh, that pace should feel like an 8.

What worked on the run: cadence at 174 average (good for an Ironman-focused athlete), running power consistent through the half marathon distance, and the ability to hold form without fading. The normalized pace (4:02/km equivalent when adjusted for terrain) shows no significant late-race deterioration, which is the more important thing to take from it.

Running power averaged 388W with normalized 401W — both consistent with what I have been producing in training, which tells me the run fitness transferred cleanly from the training block even through the fatigue.

---

## What the Flatness Actually Means

Feeling flat at a tune-up race five weeks before your target is a known phenomenon, not a crisis. Here is what is happening physiologically:

CTL at 140 means the body is carrying a large accumulated training load. ATL had been dropping through the taper leading into today, but not enough to fully offset five months of progressive overload. The legs know they are in the middle of something larger than this race, and they perform accordingly.

The personal records across bike and run segments are the counter-evidence to any concern about fitness. A flat body that still sets PRs is a fit body. The difference between how I felt today and how I will feel at Lake Placid is three weeks of progressive taper — ATL dropping while CTL holds, fatigue shedding while fitness stays.

---

## Going Into Lake Placid

Five weeks out. The training block continues this week, then the volume starts to come down deliberately. The last big training week is around June 28, with a structured taper beginning in early July.

What I am taking from today: the fitness is there, execution was clean, and the flatness was expected and manageable. I raced at controlled effort, did not blow up, set personal records, and finished without any injury concern. That is exactly what a tune-up race is supposed to do.

Lake Placid is a different event — longer, harder, with 2,500m of bike elevation and a full marathon off the back. But the foundation today confirms the preparation. The work from January through June adds up.
