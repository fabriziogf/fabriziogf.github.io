---
title: "How Two Blog Posts Changed the Way I Work as a PM"
---

I've been using AI tools for a while now, but in a scattered way. A chat here, a quick summary there. Nothing intentional. Then I read two posts from Anthropic — [one on product management in the AI era](https://claude.com/blog/product-management-on-the-ai-exponential) and [another on product development in the agentic era](https://claude.com/blog/product-development-in-the-agentic-era) — and realized I was leaving a lot on the table.

The first post argues for being deliberate about *which* tool you use for *which* type of work — not just "using AI," but having a clear mental model for when to switch contexts.

---

I've adopted that framework directly:

**Claude.ai is where I think.** Strategy docs, tricky stakeholder situations, pressure-testing an idea before writing it up. It's a thought partner, not an executor.

**Claude Code is where I build.** Prototypes, scripts, quick evals. If the output is code, I'm in Claude Code. The idea-to-signal loop is now measured in hours, not days.

**Cowork handles everything else.** Email, to-dos, slide decks, searching Slack history to understand why a decision was made six months ago. All the operational work that used to eat my week.

---

The second post reinforced something I already suspected but hadn't acted on: a lot of PM work that feels productive is actually just coordination overhead. The post makes the point clearly — "a spec that reads elegantly in a doc can fall apart the first time you try to build against it."

I now treat prototyping as part of the thinking process, not something that comes after. Building a rough version surfaces problems that no document review does. It's faster feedback, and more honest.

---

The first post also includes a striking data point: model capability improved roughly 41x on a specific task over 16 months. That's not incremental. A constraint that made something impractical six months ago may no longer exist.

This changed how I look at my backlog. I now revisit ideas I'd mentally shelved as too hard or too slow to build. Sometimes they still don't work. But sometimes they do, and quickly.

---

I'm still figuring out what fits my specific workflow. But having a clear framework for how to use these tools intentionally has been more useful than any individual prompt trick I've picked up.

If you're a PM using AI in a scattered way, both posts are worth reading — not for the specific tools, but for the mindset.
