---
title: "Thoughts on PMing with AI"
---

I've been using AI tools for a while now, both at work and in my personal life. It started as just a tool for research and writing, but has now evolved into a central part of my daily routine as a PM. I recently read two posts from Anthropic — [one on product management in the AI era](https://claude.com/blog/product-management-on-the-ai-exponential) and [another on product development in the agentic era](https://claude.com/blog/product-development-in-the-agentic-era) — that describe a structure framework similar to the approach that I have gradually adopted.

---

The first post defines a framework of deliberate use of different tools for different types of work:

**Claude.ai is where I think.** The tool is a thought partner where I ideate, do strategic writting, and polish stakeholder communication.

**Claude Code is where I build.** Where I write code for prototypes, scripts, and quick evals. It is ultimetaly my tool for quick feedback about product ideas.

**Cowork handles everything else.** The tool handles a lot of the operational work that used to eat my week: email, to-dos, project updates / slide decks, searching Slack history to understand why a decision was made six months ago. 

---

More broadly, the PM loop is accelerating as the AI tools we have access to become more intelligent and capable of broader coordination between multiple tasks.

This acceleration comes is two ways. One is changing what is possible. As described in the first post, model capability improved roughly 41x on a specific task over 16 months. A constraint that made something impractical six months ago may no longer exist and a PM has to constantly re-evaluate their roadmap to keep pace with this progress.

The second is that the work itself is evolving. Over the past year, a lot of the coordination work of a PM can be erased by quick prototyping: "a spec that reads elegantly in a doc can fall apart the first time you try to build against it." Prototyping becomes as part of the thinking process and building a rough version surfaces problems that no document review does. It's faster and more hoenst feedback.

---

I'm still figuring out what fits my specific workflow. But having a clear framework for how to use these tools intentionally has been useful.
