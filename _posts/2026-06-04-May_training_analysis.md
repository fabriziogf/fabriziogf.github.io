This post covers May 4 through June 4, 2026 — the final big training block before Rockford 70.3 on June 14 and, ultimately, Ironman Lake Placid on July 19. After years of triathlon, I know exactly what gets me hurt: too much volume and intensity at the same time. This month I tested that boundary deliberately, and for the first time in a while, I came out the other side healthy.

That's the real headline.

## Swim

Twelve swim sessions across the block — consistent weekly frequency at mostly A1/A2 aerobic effort, which is exactly right when the bike and run are carrying the heavy load.

Key sessions:
- **A1 and A1>A2 sets**: the backbone of the swim block — aerobic technique work at controlled intensity, repeated across the month to build background fitness without adding fatigue
- **12×150m varying intensity**: mixing speed and aerobic effort in the same set, training the ability to shift gears within a swim
- **Lipolítico and Melhor Média + A2**: fat-metabolism training and pace-quality sets, building efficiency at low-to-moderate effort
- **Progressivo A1→A2→A3**: effort-building sets reinforcing the feel of accelerating through the zones
- **Lap Swimming**: straightforward aerobic volume sessions keeping the weekly tick-over

The swim is always the sport I feel least confident about. But 12 sessions in a month with no missed weeks, all within prescribed zones, builds the background fitness that shows up on race day — even if the individual sessions don't feel dramatic.

## Bike

Cycling carried the bulk of the load this month, which is where it should be for Ironman prep. The structure alternated between:

- **Long Z2 rides** (3–5 hours): May 9 (120 km), May 16 (183 km), May 25 (104 km), May 30 (160 km). These are the foundation — aerobic base, fat adaptation, time-on-feet (or time-on-bike).
- **L2/Z4 interval sessions**: Weekly structured intervals — typically 7 × 7'30" at Z4 with short recoveries. This is where fitness actually moves forward.
- **Brick combos**: Several sessions ended with a run off the bike (T2 transitions), training the body to shift fuel systems and gait under fatigue.

The 5-hour rides are the ones that feel almost meditative when they're going well and completely break you down when they're not. This month they went well.

Total cycling volume: approximately 1,600 km over the block.

## Run

This is where the story gets interesting.

Historically, increasing run volume and intensity simultaneously has been the formula for injury for me. Tight calves, IT band issues, the usual suspects. This block I pushed both dials — harder interval sessions *and* more weekly kilometers — and stayed healthy. The difference wasn't fitness, it was discipline.

Every session was executed at prescribed effort. The easy runs were genuinely easy. The Z2 runs didn't drift into Z3. The intervals were hard, but they stopped when they were supposed to stop. That adherence is boring to execute and hard to maintain when you're feeling good and want to push — but it's the only thing that makes volume sustainable.

Key run sessions:
- **Ironman> 5×2000m Z3>4** (three times across the block) — the defining run session of the month. 10 km of hard intervals at race-specific effort, repeated across three separate weeks. These are the sessions that build the top-end run fitness that holds when you're 30 km into an Ironman marathon.
- **Ironman> 3×3000m Z3 and 4×3000m Z3** — longer reps at slightly lower intensity. Nine to twelve kilometers of sustained Z3 work teaches the body to hold pace under accumulating fatigue.
- **Ritmo em Zona 2** (17 km) — long aerobic runs building race-specific endurance
- **2 horas bem levinho** (2 hours easy, ~24 km): genuine long runs at low intensity, the aerobic foundation beneath the interval work
- **Corridinha Fácil** sets — recovery runs that protected the hard days
- **Post-brick runs** after long bike sessions — the most specific Ironman prep you can do

Total run volume: approximately 200 km across 19 sessions.

## Strength

Six strength sessions across the month, roughly once every five days. The sessions follow a full-body circuit structure with an emphasis on injury prevention work first: minibands, core activation, then compound movements (squats, Romanian deadlifts, chest press, rows).

Strength training for endurance athletes is often the first thing dropped when volume spikes. Keeping it in the schedule — even shortened sessions — maintains the neuromuscular base that keeps you running efficiently when you're tired.

---

## Fitness Metrics at End of Block

A CTL of 140 represents a high level of accumulated fitness. For reference, most competitive age-groupers race Ironman with a CTL between 80–120. Being at 140 with a TSB of +22 means I've done the work and I'm currently fresh — the ideal combination heading into a tune-up race.

ATL dropping while CTL holds is exactly what a taper is supposed to look like. The body is holding onto the fitness while shedding the fatigue.

---

## Going Into Rockford 70.3 (June 14)

Rockford is a tune-up, not a target race. The goal is to execute a clean race at controlled effort — not to chase a PR at the expense of Lake Placid prep. Racing a 70.3 at 85–90% effort while holding form is valuable: it's race-specific stress without the recovery cost of going all-out.

What I'll be watching: swim exit positioning, T1/T2 execution, bike pacing discipline (it's easy to go too hard at a 70.3 when legs feel fresh), and running off the bike with good form rather than grinding.

---

## What's Next

Lake Placid is 45 days away. The next block is about sharpening: keeping CTL close to where it is, letting ATL continue to drop, and doing some race-specific intensity without digging a new hole. A full Ironman taper typically starts 2–3 weeks out, which puts the last big training week around June 28.

The fact that I'm going into this final phase healthy, fit, and not carrying any injury baggage is — genuinely — more than I could say after some previous training blocks. That's the win from May.
